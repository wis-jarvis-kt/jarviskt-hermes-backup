## Core Philosophy
> "The vital few inputs to get the most outputs"

A great webinar **teaches and sells simultaneously**. Every teaching point must move the audience emotionally and logically toward the purchase decision. Content is never just content — it's a carefully engineered conversion engine.

---

## Primary Framework: MECHANISMS

The core content structure Jason uses. Each "mechanism" is a key concept/step taught using this exact structure:

### For Each Mechanism:
1. **WHAT** — What it is, what's involved, what's at stake
2. **WHY** — Why it's important, why they're limited without it, why it works
3. **HOW** — Step by step OR factors to consider
4. **TIE DOWN** — Commitment, exclamation mark comment, conclusion, challenge

> ⚠️ Red flag: If a webinar skips the WHAT-WHY-HOW-TIEDOWN structure for any key teaching point, it's leaving money on the table.

## Presentation Rules

### Present as a Roadmap
- Content must feel like a clear journey with visible milestones
- Audience should always know where they are and where they're going

### Flexible Structure, Not Rigid Formula
- Use as a guide, not a script
- Adapt based on audience's frame of reference

---

## Audience Psychology Rules

### Frame of Reference Test
- Does your audience have real experience with what you're teaching?
- **If YES** → Get granular and specific (shared experience)
- **If NO** → Use analogies, metaphors and repetition (build the frame first)

### Open Loops Enhance Learning
- Teach some → reveal more will come later → switch gears
- Creates curiosity, keeps engagement high throughout

### Action in the Mind = Purchase at Close
- Get them mentally doing the thing before the close
- e.g., "What would your ad look like if..." — mental rehearsal = buying intent

### Commitments
- "Can you see if we did just that, you'd already ______?"
- "If we stopped the webinar right now, would you agree you've gotten a ton of value?"
- Get micro-commitments throughout — they compound toward the sale

## Power Techniques

### Dramatic Demonstrations
- Do what you teach WHILE teaching it
- The "big reveal" (show results first, explain how later)
- Show great examples → later reveal they're part of what they get access to

### Relative Comparison
- Show how bad it is NOW vs. what it could be
- Give two options only — control their focus from "everything" to "this or that"
- Comparison = perceived value increase

### The Rule of 3
- Show 3 ways to get the same result
- "One they don't register, two they don't remember, three they get"
- Especially effective for new/foreign concepts

### Screenshots Are Your Best Friend
- "A picture is worth a thousand dollars"
- Scan for screenshot opportunities — proof, results, comparisons

### Anger is a Hell of a Motivator
- Calibrate to their pain — what frustrates them, what's unfair
- Use anger strategically to create urgency and desire for change

### Analogies & Metaphors
- When audience has no frame of reference, analogies are essential
- Examples: "Apartment rentals", "Profit Pie", "Kick the Giant", "Speeding vs Murder"

### Stories — Short "Slice of Life"
- Keep stories short and punchy
- Must connect to the mechanism being taught

### The Ramp Engine
- Build momentum — each mechanism should feel more exciting than the last
- Energy should ramp UP toward the close, not fade

### Make Things Specific
- "The 4 steps" / "2 types of buyers" / "Focus on these 3 things"
- Specificity = credibility = trust = sales

### Steps 1-6 Still Apply Within Content
- Hooks, calibrate to pain, tease, excite, position — even within individual mechanisms

## Step 1: HOOK Framework

> "I spend a lot of time on the hook. I try to create the hook before the product or the webinar."

### What's a Hook?
A hook must do ALL of these simultaneously:
- **Gains immediate attention** — stops the scroll/distraction
- **Gains favorable attention** — not just any attention, the right kind
- **Gains maximum qualified attention** — from the people most likely to buy
- **Increases eventual purchasing** — the hook sets up the sale from second one

### Hook Elements (the ingredients)
Novel, Shocking, Engaging, Controversial, Specific, Exciting, Dramatic, Unpredictable, Bold, Emotional, Urgent, Topical

### The 7 Key Hook Types

**#1 POP QUIZ**
- Interactive, fast-paced
- Addresses objections and makes claims more believable than just stating them
- Benefits embedded in the questions themselves
- Can be delayed (doesn't have to be first thing)

**#2 PITCH UP FRONT**
- Safer, easier, unique
- "Impossible unless…" framing
- Can be the whole frame of the webinar
- Can also be delayed

**#3 FORTUNE TELLER**
- Universal — everyone wants to know their future
- Simple to execute
- Sets up the sale naturally
- Pre-handles objections by predicting their situation

**#4 HYPE BUT TRUE!?**
- "Hype is amoral" — the bigger the claim, the more attention
- Creates intrigue + clickbait energy
- Key: the hype must be TRUE — verified by content

**#5 TALL TASK**
- Dramatic, mission-based
- Attacks resistance head-on
- Everyone loves a challenge — positions speaker as taking on something hard FOR the audience

**#6 IMMEDIATE EXPECTATION**
- Shared experience, topical
- "A lot to cover" energy — creates urgency
- Sets the tone for the whole session
- Sometimes it's okay to "go for the jugular"

**#7 BENEFIT, BENEFIT, BENEFIT**
- Done > Better (what they'll be able to do)
- Hope then pain
- Urgent tone
- Sometimes it's okay to "go for the jugular"

### Additional "Edge Case" Hooks
| Hook | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Pain, Pain, Pain** | Lead with what hurts most — for high pain audiences |
| **Left Field** | Unexpected angle that creates curiosity |
| **Intriguing Question** | Open question that demands an answer |
| **Pressure Cooker** | Scenario that creates urgency/tension immediately |
| **Recent Development** | Timely news/event as the hook |
| **Fill in the Blanks** | Audience completes the thought — engagement device |
| **Keep It Between Us** | Exclusivity/insider framing |

### How to Create Hooks
1. **Swipe from proven hooks** — adapt, don't reinvent
2. **Seek context-specific** angles — what's unique to this audience right now?
3. **Combine hook "parts"** — elements from multiple types
4. **Work backwards** — have the hook before you build the content

> ⭐ Jason's best practice: *"I had a hook before I had a product"* — the hook drives everything

### Critical Notes
- Hooks are **more important for the replay** than the live webinar
- The hook to get them TO the webinar doesn't have to be the same as the webinar's opening hook
- **Calibration** — hook must match audience awareness level
- When starting out, keep it simple

### Scoring Criteria (Hook Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Immediate Attention** | Did the first 60 seconds grab attention? |
| **Hook Type** | Which of the 7 types was used? Was it appropriate? |
| **Hook Elements** | How many elements present (novel, shocking, specific, urgent, etc.)? |
| **Qualified Attention** | Did the hook attract the RIGHT people or just anyone? |
| **Sets Up Sale** | Does the hook naturally point toward the offer? |
| **Specificity** | Was the hook specific or vague? |
| **Emotional Pull** | Did it create emotional engagement immediately? |

### Red Flags
- Generic opening ("Welcome everyone, thanks for joining...")
- Hook with no emotional charge — flat, informational start
- Big claim with no payoff — hype that isn't earned
- Hook disconnected from the offer — attention grabbed but then lost
- No urgency in the opening
- Took more than 3 minutes to get to the actual hook
- Hook too complex — tried to combine too much, lost clarity

## Step 2: PAIN Framework

> "The pain to stay the same needs to be greater than the pain to change."

### The Core Principle
> "Most people are too carrot and not enough stick. The stick is more motivating."

**Jane** (crippling toothache) will spend more money faster than **Joe** (wants a brighter smile). Pain drives buying more than desire.

### Pain Simplified — 6 Categories
1. **Excuses** — what they tell themselves why they can't
2. **Limiting Beliefs** — the false stories they believe
3. **Complaints** — what they vent about constantly
4. **Obstacles** — what blocks their progress daily
5. **Enemies** — who/what they blame
6. **Fears** — what terrifies them about the future

### Deep Audience Pain Research (12 Questions)
These reveal the full emotional landscape of the audience:

| Question | What to Uncover |
|----------|----------------|
| **Who/what terrifies them?** | Uncertainty, things that could be taken away, past traumas, where they feel powerless |
| **What/who angers them?** | Injustices, who they blame, violated trust, cheated feelings |
| **What are they desperate for?** | Hanging by a thread, what problem solves 1000 others, what they've lost |
| **What daily obstacles?** | Where stuck, common excuses, what they beat themselves up about |
| **Repetitive thoughts?** | Feeling like a failure, unworthy, incapable, regrets |
| **Recent disruptions?** | Scandals, news cycles, political/social change, tech disruption |
| **Secret desires?** | What they want but won't admit — "if I could wave a magic wand..." |
| **Thinking biases?** | Contrarian views, black-and-white thinking, decision patterns |
| **Their jargon?** | Their own labels, insider vocabulary, specific putdowns |
| **What would improve their life immediately?** | One step forward for newfound hope, certainty amid chaos |
| **What do they complain about?** | Constantly, bores them, neglecting |
| **Who are their heroes?** | Who they follow, spend money with, would fight for |

### The 8 Pain Strategies

**#1 Challenges You Face**
- External + Internal + Commitment
- External events create internal states — address both
- Get commitment to move forward IN SPITE of challenges

**#1B Exaggerated Cynicism with a Dash of Hope**
- Confrontational + Inspirational + Accountability
- People hold onto a glimmer of hope against insurmountable odds
- Provide that hope after the cynicism hits

**#2 Contrast Present to Future**
- Current limitations vs. future aspirations
- "Pain cuts twice when the opposing pleasure is well known"
- Value only understood when compared to something else

**#3 Misconceptions**
- Indirect, nuanced approach
- "If I tell you limitations about someone like you, you're less likely to get defensive"
- Indirect pain — still highly influential without triggering resistance

**#4 External Pressures**
- Specific events (real or proxied) — more immediate impact
- States are abstract, events are concrete
- Unmet expectations as the pain lever

**#5 Past Struggles**
- Near past, mid past, disruptions
- "Never let a good crisis go to waste"
- Use their history against their inaction

**#6 The Enemy**
- Blame + suppression + revenge
- "Hatred is a stronger short-term motivator than love"
- Give them someone/something to blame — then offer the solution

**#7 Anger**
- Lack of control, violations, lies
- Disruption viewed as annoyance — reframe as opportunity
- Harness anger toward the solution

**#8 Informed Pessimism**
- The 4-stage journey: Uninformed Optimism → Informed Pessimism → Crisis of Meaning → Informed Optimism
- Address the "crash and burn" phase — they're in stage 2, you're leading them to stage 4

### Critical Rules

**Calibration is Everything**
- Too much pain = no hope = paralysis = no purchase
- Not enough pain = no desire to change = no urgency
- The higher the price, the MORE pain needed

**A Tale of Two Pains**
- Short-term pain = doing something different (buying)
- Long-term pain = doing nothing, slowly descending
- Make the long-term pain feel MORE painful than the short-term action

**The Flip Practice**
- Every benefit should have a corresponding pain point
- "Each time there is a benefit, is there also a pain point?"

**Pain Placement**
- Pain baked into hooks (Pop Quiz, Fortune Teller, Tall Task)
- Pain most useful **during the close** — resurface it before the offer
- Must be front and center, not buried

### Scoring Criteria (Pain Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Pain Present Early** | Was pain established in the first 15 minutes? |
| **Strategy Used** | Which of the 8 strategies? Appropriate for audience? |
| **Calibration** | Right amount — enough urgency, enough hope? |
| **Enemy Identified** | Was there a clear "villain" (enemy, system, circumstance)? |
| **Present vs Future Contrast** | Did they contrast where audience is vs. where they could be? |
| **Pain at Close** | Was pain resurface just before the offer? |
| **The Flip Used** | For each benefit, was a corresponding pain surfaced? |
| **Long-term Pain** | Was the cost of inaction made vivid? |

### Red Flags
- All carrots, no sticks — pure aspiration with no pain
- Pain too abstract — not specific to this audience
- Pain without hope — audience paralysed, not motivated
- No enemy or obstacle identified — nothing to fight against
- Pain front-loaded only, disappears before the close
- High-ticket offer without sufficient pain established
- Audience left feeling bad with no path forward
- The mischaracterised interpretation: "find the weakness, open the wound, pour salt in, sell the treatment" — manipulative, avoid

## Step 3: TEASE Framework

> "Good strategies often accomplish multiple things — while it's good to study in isolation, remember the real world is not the laboratory"

### Core Principle
Teasing = creating open loops that compel the audience to stay. **Multiple open loops running simultaneously** — reveal some but not all. Appear to reveal all… but not quite.

### The 6 Tease Techniques

**#1 ON THIS WEBINAR**
- Easy, straight-forward
- Create AFTER the content is built (so you know what to tease)
- Uses "blind bullets" — the most versatile tool in a marketer's toolkit
- What they'll discover, without telling them exactly how

**#2 QUESTIONS**
- Faux interaction — seemingly interactive and thought-provoking
- Tweaks insecurities to gain attention
- A clever way to ship "on this webinar" content
- Creates mental engagement before content even starts

**#3 PARTIAL REVEAL**
- More specific than "on this webinar" but still leaves much to be desired
- Good for nuance, minimizes objections
- Can be "hypothetical" — "what if you could..."
- Trickier to pull off but more riveting when done right

**#4 OPEN MIND**
- For biggest claims, saturated markets, addresses inbuilt biases
- Aimed at most cynical audiences
- Also creates commitments — "just keep an open mind..."

**#5 MY GOAL**
- From here to there — sets up the transformation journey
- "Rise to the occasion" positioning
- For advanced audiences who are excited to follow the leader
- Works when speaker is already positioned as authority

**#6 FRICTION**
- Seeming contradictions, conflict (resolution), push-pull
- "Properly harnessed tension can create the most intense demands"
- Think seduction — attraction through resistance
- Most powerful but hardest to execute

### Additional Tease Tactics

**"It Gets Better"**
- Punchline is the setup — create anticipation for what's coming
- Used to re-engage mid-webinar when energy dips

**"Before We Dive In"**
- Easy open loop — signals valuable content is coming
- Creates a mini-tease before the main tease

**Teasing the Mechanisms**
- Push-pull-payoff structure
- Hint at what mechanisms will teach, then deliver

**Analogy**
- Lay the foundation with a relatable comparison
- Makes the unfamiliar feel familiar and exciting

**Giveaway**
- Least favourite technique (Jason's own note)
- Used to reward early attention

### Structure Rules
- Tease is **baked into the hook** — not separate from it
- **Teasing should occur throughout** — not just the intro
- **Master transitions**: "First though...", "Does it get better?", "More on that in a second..."
- Best developed in **second+ drafts** — hard to tease what you haven't built yet

### How to Tease
1. Run **multiple open loops** simultaneously
2. **Reveal some but not all**
3. **Appear to reveal all… but not quite**
4. **Combine techniques** as needed

### Scoring Criteria (Tease Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Open Loops Created** | How many open loops were opened in the first 15 min? |
| **Technique Used** | Which of the 6? Was it appropriate for the audience? |
| **Baked Into Hook** | Was teasing part of the opening, not an afterthought? |
| **Throughout Webinar** | Did teasing continue mid-session or only at the start? |
| **Transitions** | Smooth transitions that maintain tease momentum? |
| **Partial Reveal Quality** | Specific enough to intrigue, vague enough to keep listening? |
| **Friction Used** | Was push-pull tension used to increase desire? |

### Red Flags
- Only one open loop — not enough to hold attention
- Full reveal too early — nothing left to stay for
- No teasing after the intro — all front-loaded
- Tease completely disconnected from the offer
- "On this webinar" used generically without specificity
- No transitions between topics — abrupt shifts kill tease momentum

## Step 4: EXCITE Framework

> "Promises, benefits, outcomes, hope! Tease is a specific form of excite."

### Feature → Benefit → Outcome Chain (Use ALL Three)
| Layer | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Feature** | What it is / what it does | "Make more money" |
| **Benefit** | How it changes your life | "Never have to look at a price tag again" |
| **Outcome** | The deeper life result | The picture of the family hanging on the wall |

> ⚠️ Red flag: Stopping at Feature or Benefit — never reaching the emotional Outcome level.

### The 7 Excite Techniques

**#1 EXPLOIT**
- Power reversal, take advantage of, "a real secret"
- "It shouldn't be possible under ordinary conditions"
- Creates the feeling of an unfair advantage

**#2 RESULTS**
- Quantity AND quality of proof
- Visual results (screenshots, income, numbers)
- Claim validation — "everywhere you look someone is winning!"

**#3 BLING**
- Home, car, toys, travel — tangible success markers
- Most superficial but very effective
- Objects and destinations demonstrate success visually

**#4 EASY**
- Little effort for result, no experience needed
- "Bad > nothing" — imperfect action beats no action
- Key insight: *"People would rather put in almost no effort for an okay result than a lot of effort for a big result"*

**#5 ASPIRATION**
- Purpose, higher self, motivation
- Connects to identity and potential
- "You have within you infinite potential"

**#6 STORY**
- Easy to take in, entertaining, "happily ever after"
- Stories bypass resistance — Jesus told stories
- Must connect the audience to the desired future state

**#7 QUICK**
- Instant gratification, diminished risk, reason/excuse
- *"Smaller results sooner are better than bigger results later"*

**#7 TIMING** *(bonus technique)*
- The ultimate exploit — urgency built in
- "You just happen to be at the right place at the right time"
- Real factor of success framing

### Additional Excite Techniques
- **Before we dive in** — mini preview of value coming
- **It gets better** — escalating promise
- **Tease the mechanism** — hint at HOW without revealing
- **Analogy** — make the abstract tangible
- **Giveaway** — immediate value delivery

### Facts & Stats Technique
> Wild Claim → Stat to back it → Tie down

### Testimonials
- Social proof that validates the excite claims
- Shows real people winning (not just the guru)

### Rules for Excite
1. **Must be in the intro** — excite early, not late
2. **Push to the edge of hype** — but don't cross it
3. **Use at least one technique** — never skip excite entirely
4. **Garnish the rest** — excite woven throughout, not just front-loaded

### Ongoing Use
- Excitement continues throughout the webinar, not just intro
- **Push-pull** is key: enhance excitement without becoming manic
- Carrot AND stick — desire AND pain — keep both alive

### Scoring Criteria (Excite Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Feature-Benefit-Outcome** | Does it reach the emotional OUTCOME level or stop at feature? |
| **Excite Technique Used** | Which of the 7 techniques? At least 1 required |
| **In the Intro** | Was excite present in the first 10 minutes? |
| **Results Shown** | Quantity + quality of proof visible/mentioned? |
| **Easy Framing** | Was the effort required made to feel minimal? |
| **Story Used** | Was there at least one compelling story? |
| **Push-Pull Balance** | Was excitement sustained without going manic? |
| **Timing/Urgency** | Was "right time right place" framing used? |

### Red Flags
- Opening with no excitement — flat, business-like start
- Only features, no benefits or outcomes reached
- No proof/results shown (no social validation)
- Aspiration missing — no connection to higher purpose
- Story absent — all logic, no emotion
- Excitement front-loaded only, disappears mid-webinar

## Step 8: COMMITMENT Framework

> "It's ideal to have a powerful commitment that puts an exclamation mark on the end of the content and then best queues up the offer to be made!"

### Where Commitments Live (4 Locations)
1. **Within the mechanisms** — after each WHAT-WHY-HOW, a tie-down commitment
2. **Before a point is made** — front-loaded commitment that primes agreement
3. **To tie a point down** — seal the insight after delivering it
4. **In the intro** — early commitment sets the frame for the entire webinar

### Commitment Progression
```
INTRO COMMITMENTS
    ↓
COMMITMENT BEFORE CONTENT (primes agreement)
    ↓
COMMITMENTS THROUGHOUT (within each mechanism)
    ↓
ADVANCED COMMITMENT (between content and offer) ← MOST CRITICAL
    ↓
OFFER
```

### The Advanced Commitment (Between Content & Offer)
This is the **most powerful commitment** — it sits at the critical transition point between content and offer. Its job:
- Put an **exclamation mark** on the end of the content
- **Queue up the offer** — make the close feel like a natural next step, not a sales pitch
- Lock in all the value delivered before asking for money

> Example language: "Can you see how if you just did [X], you'd already [result]?" / "If we stopped right now, would you agree you've already gotten massive value?"

### Scoring Criteria (Commitment Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Intro Commitment** | Was there an early commitment in the first 10 min? |
| **Pre-Point Commitments** | Were commitments used before making key points? |
| **Mechanism Tie-Downs** | Was each teaching point sealed with a commitment? |
| **Advanced Commitment** | Was there a powerful commitment between content and offer? |
| **Natural Flow to Offer** | Did the commitment make the close feel inevitable? |
| **Commitment Stacking** | Did micro-commitments build toward macro-commitment? |

### Red Flags
- No commitment before the offer — cold close feels jarring
- Tie-downs missing after mechanisms — insights not locked in
- Advanced commitment skipped — direct jump from content to selling
- Commitments feel forced or manipulative rather than natural
- Only one commitment in the whole webinar — not enough stacking
- Offer presented before audience has committed to the value

## Step 5: POSITION Framework

> "The position of the point is more important than the point itself!"

### Two Positions to Establish

**Position 1 — YOU**
> "How you're uniquely qualified to solve this problem. They can't solve this from anyone else. Either they solve it through you or they are hopeless."

**Position 2 — THE OPPORTUNITY**
> "Why it's hot, why it needs to be done NOW, why others who try the normal way will fall short, why YOUR specific approach is the difference between winning or not."

### 5 Ways to Position Yourself

**1. Proof** — Results are the strongest position
- Direct results (your own wins)
- Secondary results (byproducts of your work)
- Customer results (testimonials)
- Accolades (recognition, press, credibility markers)
- Visuals (screenshots, income proof, before/after)
- Key insight: *"Quantity becomes its own quality"* — enough proof overwhelms doubt

**2. Motive** — Why you're doing this, why you care
- Authentic reason creates trust
- Motive answers "why are you telling me this?"

**3. Analogy** — Make your expertise relatable
- Compare your expertise to something familiar
- Lowers resistance, increases understanding

**4. Story** — Your origin/transformation story
- The journey that makes you the guide
- Must connect your past to their current situation

**5. Niched Down** — Specificity creates authority
- The more specific your positioning, the more credible

### 4 Levels of Self-Positioning

| Level | Type | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Level 1** | MVP | Minimum viable positioning — quick and simple |
| **Level 2** | Quick & Great | Efficient positioning that establishes credibility fast |
| **Level 3** | Big Bopper | Full authority positioning with multiple proof elements |
| **Level 4** | Inception | The deepest positioning — the audience becomes the proof |

### How to Apply
1. **Find a position you can take, hold and extend**
2. Ideally a position that **crushes competition AND enhances desire**
3. Assert that position with **as many elements dramatically deployed** as possible
4. **Engineer the position** if possible — don't just claim it, design it

### Critical Rule
> "How you speak and carry yourself matters!" — Positioning isn't just what you say, it's how you say it. Tonality, confidence, pace all signal authority.

### Scoring Criteria (Position Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **You Positioned** | Was the speaker clearly positioned as THE authority? |
| **Opportunity Positioned** | Was the timing/urgency of the opportunity established? |
| **Proof Used** | Direct results, customer results, accolades, visuals? |
| **Quantity of Proof** | Enough proof to overwhelm doubt? |
| **Motive Clear** | Was there a genuine "why" behind the speaker's presence? |
| **Story Used** | Origin story or transformation narrative? |
| **Niched Down** | Was positioning specific or generic? |
| **Delivery Confidence** | Tone and body language matching the claimed authority? |
| **Competition Addressed** | Why this solution vs. others? |

### Red Flags
- No clear authority establishment — audience doesn't know why to trust the speaker
- Generic credentials ("I've helped many people") instead of specific proof
- No opportunity positioning — why NOW? not established
- Proof shown without context — numbers without meaning
- Story too long — positioning takes more than 10 minutes (loses audience)
- Motive missing — speaker seems to be selling just to make money
- Confidence/delivery doesn't match claimed authority level
- No differentiation from alternatives the audience might consider

## Step 6: PARADIGM Framework

> "The conclusion you want your audience to draw that is most conducive to purchasing the product you sell"

### The Goal
Create an **"A-HA! This changes everything!"** moment — a belief shift so powerful the audience can't go back to their old way of thinking.

> "What's the belief that makes it impossible to go back to the old, dumb, limiting way of doing things? That keeps them awake at night until they buy?"

### Two Types of Paradigm
- **Stated** — directly told to the audience
- **Implied** — audience draws the conclusion themselves (often more powerful)

### Core Rule
> The paradigm not only gives insight but **makes desire of ownership increase massively**

### Proven Paradigm Templates

| Paradigm | Core Idea |
|----------|-----------|
| **Show the Manual, Sell the Automatic** | Teach them HOW to do it manually → they think "WOW this is great but a lot of work — I wish I could do it!" → sell the automated version |
| **Small Results Now > Big Results Later** | Immediate small wins are more valuable than promised future big results |
| **Speed as a Paradigm** | Cut time down significantly → people pay more and are easier to sell |
| **If This is Free, Paid Must Be Insane** | Give so much value in free content that paid feels like a no-brainer |
| **Even a Loser Like Me Can Do It** | Relatability + accessibility — removes the "I'm not good enough" objection |
| **Get In On the Ground Floor** | Early adopter positioning — exclusivity + timing urgency |
| **A Big Fat Aggressive Promise** | Works well when you have ample proof — big bold claim + evidence |
| **Launch in Beta** | Soft launch framing — creates exclusivity + early adopter status |

### Paradigms Can Be Combined
Example (Launchpad):
- Paradigm 1: Show the manual, sell the automatic
- Paradigm 2: A big fat aggressive promise
- Paradigm 3: Launch in "beta"

### Key Principle
> "Paradigms meet the market where it's at"

The paradigm must match the audience's current awareness level and dominant frustration.

> "The consciousness that caused the problem can't solve it" — the paradigm IS the shift from old consciousness to new.

### Scoring Criteria (Paradigm Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **A-HA Moment** | Was there a clear belief-shift moment? Did audience visibly/audibly react? |
| **Paradigm Type Used** | Which template(s)? Were they appropriate for this audience? |
| **Manual → Automatic Setup** | If applicable — did they demo the hard way before revealing the easy way? |
| **Desire Increase** | After the paradigm, did desire for the product clearly increase? |
| **Paradigm Seeded Early** | Was the paradigm set up early and paid off later? |
| **Impossibility of Going Back** | After the shift, would it feel stupid to NOT buy? |
| **Combo Used** | Were multiple paradigms layered for maximum effect? |

### Red Flags
- No clear "this changes everything" moment — just teaching with no belief shift
- Paradigm disconnected from the offer (audience has the shift but doesn't connect it to buying)
- Too abstract — paradigm not grounded in specific proof or demonstration
- Stated paradigm only, never implied (weaker sell)
- Market sophistication mismatch — paradigm pitched too high or too low

## Step 9: TRANSITION Framework

> The bridge between content and close. Done wrong, the audience feels ambushed. Done right, buying feels like the obvious next step.

### The Basic Formula (3 Steps)
1. **Recap** — Show all the cool stuff as quickly and dramatically as possible
2. **Customer Results** — Back up every claim with proof from real customers
3. **Transition to Close** — Use one of the transition scripts

### Why This Step Exists
The transition is where the emotional state built during content gets *carried* into the close. Without it, there's a jarring gear shift from "learning" to "buying" that triggers resistance.

### The Two Choices Frame
One of the key transition scripts — present the audience with exactly two options:
- **Option A:** Do nothing (stay where they are, accept the pain)
- **Option B:** Take action with you (the solution being offered)

Makes the decision binary and simple. Eliminates "maybe later."

### Key Principles
- **Quick and dramatic recap** — not a slow re-run, a highlight reel that re-excites
- **Customer results** carry the proof — social validation at the most critical moment
- **Transition scripts** provide the verbal bridge — the exact words that move from content to offer naturally

### Scoring Criteria (Transition Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Recap Present** | Was there a quick, exciting recap of content value? |
| **Customer Results Shown** | Was proof shown right at the transition point? |
| **Smooth Bridge** | Did the shift to selling feel natural or jarring? |
| **Two Choices Frame** | Was the binary choice presented clearly? |
| **Emotional State Maintained** | Was excitement/momentum from content carried into close? |
| **Speed** | Was the recap fast and punchy, not slow? |

### Red Flags
- No recap — jumping straight from last mechanism to price
- Transition too slow — loses momentum built in content
- No customer results at transition — proof gap before close
- Awkward gear shift — audience can feel the "now I'm going to sell you" moment
- Two choices not presented — audience left with open-ended decision
- Energy drops during transition — excitement evaporates before close begins

## Step 10: OFFER Framework

> "Every great offer needs THIS slide!" — The offer is engineered, not assembled.

### The Offer Building Process
1. **Think of everything you COULD offer** — brainstorm without limits
2. **Assign highest value to the right components** — what moves the needle most?
3. **Right size it** — not too much, not too little
4. **Decide core vs. bonus** — what's in the main offer vs. what's a bonus
5. **Figure out the order** — sequence matters for perceived value buildup

### 8 Criteria for Each Offer Component
Every element of the offer should score well on:
1. **Easy to explain and understand** — complexity kills conversions
2. **Demonstrable** — can you show it working?
3. **High retail value** — what would it cost to get this elsewhere?
4. **High perceived value** — does it FEEL valuable to the buyer?
5. **Novelty** — is it something they haven't seen before?
6. **Objection crushing** — does it address a specific reason not to buy?
7. **Time and money cost to deliver** — justifies the price
8. **Scale** — can you deliver this to many people?

### Offer Parts (The Stack)
- **Product name** — must be compelling, specific, memorable
- **Story** — why this offer exists, the origin of what's inside
- **Make up** — what's actually included, spelled out with drama

### The Drama Rule
> Sprinkle in drama and all the other elements learned throughout — the offer is a performance, not a list.

### Key Principle: "Every Great Offer Needs THIS Slide"
The offer slide must visually anchor the value stack — showing everything included, each with its value, building to the total before the price reveal.

### Scoring Criteria (Offer Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Offer Clarity** | Easy to understand what's included? |
| **Value Stack Built** | Was the perceived value accumulated before price? |
| **Demonstrable Components** | Were key elements shown working, not just described? |
| **Novelty** | Was there something surprising/unique in the offer? |
| **Objection Crushing** | Did specific components address known objections? |
| **Drama Used** | Was the offer presented with energy and theater? |
| **Product Name** | Compelling and specific or generic? |
| **Story Behind Offer** | Was there a reason WHY this offer exists? |
| **Right Sized** | Felt like the right amount — not overwhelming or thin? |

### Red Flags
- Offer presented as a simple price list — no drama, no value building
- No value stack — price revealed before perceived value established
- Components hard to understand — complexity kills at the close
- Generic product name — no specificity or intrigue
- No story behind the offer — feels transactional
- Objections not addressed by offer components
- Components not demonstrable — audience has to take it on faith
- Offer feels either too thin (not enough) or too bloated (confused)

## Step 12: BONUSES Framework

> "Your best stuff should be in your bonuses" — More important than the core offer components, so spend more time on them!

### Core Principle
- In affiliate offers, barely mention the course — it's linked to everything shown in content
- Scarcity is intertwined into pretty much every bonus
- Bonuses need sexy headlines, bullet points, drama — same as (or better than) core offer

### 14 Bonus Deliverable Types
1. **Training** — Interviews, recordings, PDFs, live calls
2. **Templates** — Done-for-you frameworks

7. **Software** — Tools, apps
8. **Live Events** — Tickets, attendance
9. **Alongsides** — "Alongside you" access — they do it WITH you
10. **Combos** — Bundled deliverables
11. **Libraries** — Vaults of resources
12. **Swipes** — Copy, scripts, templates to steal
13. **Case Studies** — Proof-based walkthroughs
14. **SOPs** — Standard operating procedures

### Bonus Presentation Strategies
| Strategy | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Testimonial for Bonus Package** | Social proof specifically for the bonuses |
| **Speed** | How fast they'll get results with the bonus |
| **Cool Sneak Peek** | Preview of the bonus content to create desire |

| **NDA Bonus Mystery** | Can't reveal it — creates intrigue |

### Additional Considerations
- **Multiple modalities** — variety of bonus types keeps interest high
- **Build around proof** — bonuses anchored to results/testimonials

### Scoring Criteria (Bonuses Lens)

- No "best for last" — strongest bonus revealed first (kills momentum)

## Step 13: RISK Framework

> "Show how it's riskier to say NO than yes. And be willing to spend hours handling objections — that's where all the money is!"

### Two Components
1. **Objections** — address the universal and specific reasons not to buy
2. **Guarantees** — remove the financial/emotional risk of saying yes

### Guarantee Types
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Unconditional Money Back** | No questions asked — lowest barrier |
| **Conditional Money Back** | Must do X to qualify (protects from refund abuse) |
| **Conditional Full Pay Only** | Guarantee only if paid in full |
| **Better Than Money Back** | Keep bonuses + get refund / extra value if not satisfied |
| **Simple** | Short, clear, no complexity |
| **Easy Conditions** | Conditions exist but are easy to meet |
| **Named Guarantee** | Give the guarantee a compelling name |
| **No Guarantee** | Sometimes works when positioning is ultra-premium |

> Key: **Selling the guarantee** — don't just state it, dramatize it. The guarantee itself is a selling tool.

### 5 Universal Objections (Must Handle All)
1. **The Money Objection** — "I can't afford it"
2. **The Time Objection** — "I don't have time"
3. **The Confidence/Competence Objection** — "I don't think I can do this"
4. **The Alternatives Objection** — "What about [competitor]?"
5. **The Procrastination Objection** — "I'll think about it / maybe later"

Plus **product-specific objections** unique to the offer.

### Stages of a Prospect (5 Levels)
1. "No one can do it"
2. "You can do it — but that's it"
3. "You and some other people can do it"
4. "You and most others can do it"
5. "Okay, I'll try to do it" ← goal

> **Testimonials are the antidote to most risks** — social proof moves people through these stages faster than any argument.

### Key Objection Scripts

**Fear of Failure:**
- "A child doesn't let fear of falling stop them from learning to ride a bike... if we have to pick you up and put you back on your feet a couple of times, that's okay."
- "Let fear come along for the ride — just put it in the passenger seat."

**Suspicion:**
- "Some think this too good to be true — I say it's too good to be false."
- "Don't believe everything I say. Just believe enough to try it out."

**Commitment:**
- "We're all faced with two pains — the pain of regret or the pain of commitment. The pain of commitment weighs ounces, the pain of regret weighs tons."
- "You take one step, I take ten steps."

**Experience (Beginner):**
- "We were all new at one time. It's not where you start that matters, it's where you end up."
- "You can't learn to swim in a seminar. You have to get in the pool. And I'll be your lifeguard."

**Speed of Results:**
- "That's the best part! We designed it to get results ASAP."

### The Risk of Saying NO
> "Find better tables instead of getting better at poker." — Jason's philosophy: help customers avoid the wrong situations, not just overcome fear.

The most powerful close: **make inaction feel riskier than action.**

### Specific Close Types
Order Form Close, Future Close, 3 Options Close, Alternate Two Choices, "Is It For Me?", Major vs Minor, The Risk of Saying No, Competition Close, Burden Their Risk, Support Close, Ride the Bike Close, Objection Solicitation

### Key Rule: Risk Appears Early AND Late
- Risk handling **in the intro** — pre-empt objections from the start
- Risk handling **in the content section** — weave objection crushing through teaching
- Risk handling **in the close** — full dedicated objection handling
- "Always be closing" — risk handled throughout, not just at the end

### Scoring Criteria (Risk Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Guarantee Present** | Was a guarantee offered? Which type? |
| **Guarantee Sold** | Was it stated flatly or dramatized and sold? |
| **Universal Objections Addressed** | Money, time, confidence, alternatives, procrastination? |
| **Testimonials Used** | Social proof to overcome specific objections? |
| **Risk of Saying No** | Was inaction framed as the riskier choice? |
| **Scripts Used** | Were specific close scripts deployed? |
| **Early Objection Handling** | Was risk handled in intro/content, not just close? |
| **Specific Objections** | Were product-specific objections addressed? |

### Red Flags
- No guarantee — maximum buyer friction
- Guarantee stated but not sold — wasted opportunity
- Universal objections ignored — money/time/confidence left unaddressed
- No social proof at the close — testimonials only in content section
- Inaction never framed as risky — only action presented as risky
- Close attempted with no objection handling — direct pitch = low conversion
- Objection handling felt defensive — should feel empowering and confident

## Step 14: SCARCITY Framework

> "Scarcity is the greatest motivator of all time."

### 5 Types of Scarcity
1. **Price goes up** — act now or pay more later
2. **Bonuses go away** — delay = lose the extras
3. **Offer closes** — it won't be available at all
4. **Window of opportunity passes** — timing-based urgency
5. **Combination** — stack multiple scarcity types for maximum effect

### The Scarcity Process (3 Questions)
1. **What will be scarce?** — price, bonus, access, spots
2. **Why is it scarce?** — the reason must be believable
3. **How can you show it as dramatically as possible?** — proof + theater

### Reason Why (Critical — Without It, Scarcity Feels Manipulative)
Must justify the scarcity with a credible reason:
- To reward fast action takers
- Otherwise the offer isn't special
- "It's out of my hands" — external constraint
- External conditions (e.g., seats at a seminar)
- Looking for beta users
- Contractual obligation
- Pre-release pricing window

### Believability in Scarcity
- **Show the proof it's scarce** — screenshot, counter, deadline visual
- **Explain the consequences** — what specifically happens if they miss it

### The Language of Scarcity
*Limited, Short time, While it lasts, First mover advantage, Miss out, Before it's gone, Exclusive, Deadline, Early Bird, First come first served, Now, Rare*

### Scarcity as the Frame (3 Examples)
- **The Plan** — beta launch framing
- **Auction Webinar** — contractual obligation creates real scarcity
- **Prosperity Algorithm** — pre-release price

### Key Tactics
| Tactic | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Fast Action Bonus** | Extra bonus only for immediate buyers |
| **Pre-Order** | Lock in price before official launch |
| **The Catch** | "The offer expires shortly" — simple, direct |
| **Bad News** | Announce what's going away — creates urgency through loss |

### Scoring Criteria (Scarcity Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Scarcity Present** | Was any scarcity established during the close? |
| **Reason Why Given** | Was the scarcity justified with a believable reason? |
| **Type Used** | Price / bonus / access / window / combo? |
| **Proof Shown** | Was the scarcity demonstrated visually? |
| **Language Used** | Did the language create genuine urgency? |
| **Drama** | Was the scarcity revealed dramatically or just mentioned? |
| **Fast Action Bonus** | Was there a reward for immediate decision? |
| **Believability** | Did it feel real or manipulative? |

### Red Flags
- No scarcity at all — no urgency, easy to say "I'll think about it"
- Scarcity with no reason why — feels fake and manipulative
- Vague scarcity ("limited time only") with no specifics
- Scarcity announced but not demonstrated (no proof)
- Only one weak scarcity element vs. stacked urgency
- Energy drop when delivering scarcity — must be said with conviction
- Scarcity contradicted later (offer still available after "deadline")

## Step 11: PRICE Framework

> "Raising your price will probably increase conversions even though you're selling at a higher price."

### Core Price Psychology

**The Value-Price Signal:**
- If you keep adding value but never raise the price, customers assume the value HASN'T improved
- Example: ASM stayed at $3,500 for 4 versions. Jason's argument: "You're telling me it's gotten better but the price is the same? That doesn't make sense."
- Result: Raising ASM from $3,500 → $5,000 produced their biggest launch ever ($25M)
- Lesson: Price IS a signal of value. Flat price = stagnant product in the customer's mind

**The No-Man's Land Prices:**
- Price ceilings generally exist at $2,000 and $5,000
- $3,500 is "no man's land" — between two clear anchors
- $2,497 vs $2,997: psychologically no real difference — just go to $2,997
- Wholesale Formula actually converted HIGHER at $2,997 than $2,497

### Payment Plans
- If 50/50 split between full pay and payment plan → present both options as separate, clear choices
- Don't hide the payment plan — lead with whichever is more common for your audience
- Always disclose full financing terms clearly
- Key line: "Whatever option you take, you get exactly the same [product]"

### Price Testing Philosophy
- Never go backwards on price — you can never un-raise a price
- When price isn't converting: don't guess, test ONE variable at a time
- Make hypothesis → test → post-mortem → move to next variable
- "The cell that is 99% closed is worth exactly $0" — there's a very fine line

### Monetizing Bonuses (Advanced)
- Bonuses CAN be revenue streams — not just value add
- Jason's model: Group coaching bonus → give 60 days free → kick out unless they actively demand to stay → $297/month continuity → 1,500 people paying = $450k/month
- Software bonus: give lowest tier free → they upgrade when they're profitable → earn commission on upgrade
- Key principle: ethical bonuses → none of these were abusive — they required the customer to succeed before they paid more

### Product Life Cycles
- Every product has a life cycle — success eventually leads to "old and crusty" perception
- Solution: Strategic sunset + relaunch under new name/positioning/branding
- The new version should be essentially similar but with new hook, paradigm, and framing

### The Webinar Advantage
- Webinars are highly malleable vs other formats
- Can go back to same audience within days to iterate
- WP Twin example: $5k first webinar → identified FTP confusion + single demo → fixed those two things → $120k from SAME audience 4 days later
- "The difference between closing and losing is a single letter"

### Feedback & Research
- Don't ask customers directly "why didn't you buy?" — they don't know and will confuse you
- Observe behavioral patterns + language used → make inferences
- Sit on webinars and notice: "Why do they keep asking THIS question when they shouldn't be?"
- Surveys are dangerous — question framing prejudices answers

### Scoring Criteria (Price Lens)

| Area | What to Look For |
|------|-----------------|
| **Price Confidence** | Does speaker deliver price without hesitation or apology? |
| **Value-Price Alignment** | Is the price clearly BELOW the stacked value? |
| **Payment Plan Clarity** | Are options presented clearly without confusion? |
| **Price Anchoring** | Is there a higher reference price before the actual price? |
| **Price Signal** | Does the price signal quality and seriousness? |

### Red Flags
- Apologising for the price ("I know it's a lot but...")
- Price in no-man's land (e.g. $3,500 when $2,000 or $5,000 would work better)
- Hiding the payment plan — or making it confusing
- Never testing price — assuming lower = better
- Giving away too much value with flat price = devaluing the product over time
