## 1. AI Used to Resurrect Dead Pilots' Voices (NTSB Incident)

**Source:** TechCrunch, May 22, 2026  
**Link:** https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/22/ai-is-being-used-to-resurrect-the-voices-of-dead-pilots/

The National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) temporarily removed public access to its docket system after discovering that voices of pilots killed in a UPS plane crash (Flight 2976, Louisville, KY) had been reconstructed using AI and were circulating on the internet.

**What happened:**
- Federal law prohibits cockpit audio recordings from being included in the public docket
- However, the docket contained a spectrogram file (image representation of audio frequencies) from the cockpit voice recorder
- People took the spectrogram + publicly available transcript and used AI tools (including Codex) to reconstruct approximate cockpit audio
- NTSB restored public access on Friday but kept 42 investigations closed pending review — including the one for Flight 2976

**Why it matters:** This is a concrete example of AI circumventing legal protections around sensitive recordings, raising questions about how AI can reconstruct data that was intentionally restricted from public view.

## 2. Spotify & Universal Music Strike AI Cover Deal

**Source:** The Verge / TechCrunch, May 22, 2026

Spotify and Universal Music have reached an agreement that explicitly allows fan-made AI covers and remixes on the Spotify platform.

**What this means:**
- Fans can create and share AI-generated remixes of songs using artists' voices and likenesses
- The deal provides a legal framework for AI-generated music content that previously existed in a gray area
- Labels and artists get a cut of revenue from AI-generated content

**Why it matters:** This is one of the first major label deals to explicitly legitimize AI-generated music content at scale. It signals a shift from fighting AI music to licensing it — potentially reshaping how artists and labels monetize fan creativity.

## 3. Google Unveils "Omni" — An Anything-to-Anything Multimodal AI Model

**Source:** The Verge, May 22, 2026  
**Title:** "Google's new anything-to-anything AI model is wild"

Google announced a new AI model called "Omni" that can accept any input type (text, image, audio, video, etc.) and generate any output type — a significant expansion of multimodal capabilities.

**Notable capabilities demonstrated:**
- Sent a child's stuffed animal "rafting" (creative visual generation)
- Deepfaked someone in front of the Eiffel Tower
- The reviewer notes it's "not quite the singularity" but is remarkably versatile

**Why it matters:** Google's Omni model represents a push toward truly unified multimodal AI — not just understanding different input types, but freely converting between them. This could simplify pipelines that currently require multiple specialized models.
